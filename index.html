No robot on Earth can walk into an unknown kitchen and make coffee. Not because the hardware isn't ready — but because the software paradigm is broken.

Every major lab follows the same playbook: collect massive datasets, train one model per task on GPU clusters, deploy into a robot, repeat. Walking is one model. Grasping is another. Opening a door is another. Each new skill costs months of data, millions in compute, and hundreds of watts at inference. $20 billion has been poured into this approach. The result is zero general-purpose autonomous robots.

This is a dead end. The real world contains an infinity of tasks, objects, and situations. You cannot train a model for every variant.

The proof that another way exists has been in front of us all along.

The human brain runs on 20 watts. It controls 600+ muscles simultaneously with sub-millimeter precision. It processes vision, touch, balance, and sound in parallel. It learns continuously for 80 years without forgetting how to walk. It adapts to any new tool in minutes. No dataset. No GPU cluster. No reward function.

A two-year-old who has never seen a broom can still push it — not because they were trained on 10,000 videos of people sweeping, but because they have an internal model of the world.
